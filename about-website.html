About this Website

Why Learn About Cancer?

As most medical professionals will tell you, the best, most successful procedure is the one that doesn’t happen. Learning about cancer will help you identify the early signs of cancer so that you can recognize when something isn’t right, and take action sooner. The risk of cancer associated mortality gets exponentially worse the later it is caught, meaning that knowing the symptoms of cancer ensures that you are best prepared for the fight against cancer!

What is Cancer?

Cancer is uncontrolled cell growth. In a healthy body, cells replicate at a well-defined pace (called the cell cycle), ensuring that resources are properly distributed. To keep this cycle in check, the body uses various checkpoints to prevent cells from growing too much.

In cancer, these controls become inactive or are lost entirely. This allows cells to grow rapidly, often neglecting their normal responsibilities. Surprisingly, the body encounters many cancer cells daily. Most are detected and destroyed by the immune system.

However, in rare cases, cancer cells learn how to hide from the immune system. Once undetectable, the body can't destroy them because it doesn’t realize they're there. These hidden cells are then free to grow and spread, draining the body of vital resources.

Cancer can arise from any cell type at any stage of life—even embryonically. However, it occurs more often in cells that divide frequently or are exposed to environmental factors, such as skin cells.

How is Cancer Named?

The name of a cancer typically includes a Latin prefix (sometimes an infix) and a suffix. The prefix refers to the organ or cell type. If present, the infix usually identifies the tumor type. The suffix is commonly -oma, meaning “tumor.”

For example, the cancer Angiosarcoma breaks down as:

  • Angio – “vessel” (prefix)
  • Sarc – “flesh” or “soft tissue” (infix)
  • Oma – “tumor” (suffix)

Together, "Angiosarcoma" means a soft tissue tumor of the vessels, or a cancer originating in the blood or lymphatic vessels.
