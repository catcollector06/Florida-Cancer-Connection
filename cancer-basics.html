The Basics of Cancer

What Causes Cancer?

Genetic and epigenetic factors

Specific mutations in one’s genome can lead to an increase in the likelihood of developing cancer. These mutations are called Oncogenes. Proto-oncogenes are non-mutated, properly functioning genes that, when mutated, become oncogenes.

Environmental risks

The environment that one is in can also increase the likelihood of developing cancer. While one’s environment is strictly defined as the physical space one is in, there are an incredibly vast number of variables that impact the environment that one is in. For example, socioeconomic status has been correlated with higher incidence of lung, breast, prostate, and other forms of cancer as pollution, food insecurity, poor / no health insurance, and low access to care are all pervading factors.

Being aware of one’s environment is incredibly important in understanding cancer risk. Environmental factors that can lead to an increase in cancer include: air quality, education, access to healthcare, available food, Etc.

Behavioral factors

Unsurprisingly, the behaviors that one performs can impact their chances of getting cancer. Some actions, like smoking, tanning, or drinking, drastically increase your chances of developing cancer. Read more about this in the “How do you prevent cancer?” section!

Random mutations (chance)

Sometimes getting cancer is just due to plain chance. Whenever a cell divides, small mutations occur when replicating and sorting out DNA. These are usually controlled for and managed, but sometimes a cell will mutate just right, creating the perfect cellular cocktail to create cancer.

What are the Symptoms of Cancer?

General symptoms

Since cancer can impact every tissue in the body, cancers generally have symptoms that are unique to their respective organs. However, the following symptoms are associated with cancer in general, meaning that if you see or feel any of these for an extended period of time, it's definitely worth getting it screened (click here for free screening resources). Also, a more in depth checklist of symptoms organized by their organ system can be found in the Cancers of Organ System section!

  • Palpable lump in an unusual area
  • Pain / soreness for an extended period
  • Unexplainable fatigue/shortness of breath
  • Blood in places it shouldn't be (urine, stool, vomit, semen, vaginal discharge*, etc.)
    • *Note: Bloody discharge or abnormal vaginal bleeding may signal reproductive cancers. If it occurs outside menstruation or unrelated to sex, consult a doctor.
  • Significant changes in stool shape / consistency
    • Change in frequency of bowel movements
  • Weight changes (+ or -) with no significant change in eating habits

When to see a doctor

Having any one of these symptoms doesn’t necessarily mean that you have cancer. Even a lump could be a cyst or abscess rather than a tumor. However, if you feel that any one of these symptoms is abnormal for your body, trust your intuition. You know yourself best, so if you think something isn’t quite right, get it checked out.

“As I always say, you are your best doctor.” — Dr. John Kiluk, MD, FACS, Surgical Oncology, Moffitt, 2025

Screening isn’t fun, but it can save your life. Think of it like this:

While grazing, a gazelle hears a twig snap. It sees nothing, hears nothing else. It can either keep grazing or run. If it runs and it was nothing, it wasted energy. But if it stays and it was a lion—it’s lunch. The gazelle always runs. Similarly, if you notice unusual symptoms, it’s better to get checked and find nothing than to ignore it and risk cancer spreading.

How do you Prevent Cancer?

Lifestyle changes, vaccinations, and understanding your own risks

While it’s nearly impossible to tell what causes any person’s cancer, there is a clear relationship between carcinogen exposure and the development of cancer. Thus, the first thing that you can do to minimize your chances of getting cancer is understanding the risks unique to your own environment. There are a variety of behavioral and environmental factors that greatly increase one’s risk of developing cancer, including:

  • Smoking, vaping, and other tobacco products (such as dip)
  • Tanning (which is essentially UV radiation)
  • Drinking
  • Obesity / sedentary lifestyle (↑ inflammation and carcinogenic foods intake)
  • Stress (impairs immune system)
  • Radiation (X-Rays, UV radiation from the sun, etc.)

It would be outrageous to ask any person to completely stop or eliminate any of these behaviors / situations. One doesn’t just ‘unstress’ completely, and drinking is fun. But at the end of the day, any reduction of these behaviors will reduce your chance of cancer. So, you don’t have to stop drinking, but reducing your alcohol intake somewhat will decrease your chances of breast, gastrointestinal, liver and pancreatic cancers. No matter what, if you choose to reduce these behaviors in any amount (even one less drink per night!), you should be proud of the steps you’re taking to prevent cancer.
